City Planning


  • With the influx of people to urban centers came the increasingly obvious problem of city layouts.
  • Major streets were quite often the original paths beaten by wandering cows and passing travelers before the city was large.
  • During the period of Enlightenment Europe, many European rulers and kings took of the task of redesigning their cities to be more efficient,
    as well as better show of the grandeur of their nation.
  • Improvements were made in hygiene and fire safety with wider streets, stone construction and access to water sources.
  • Wider streets accounted for more foot traffic
  • Railroad tracks were driven into the heart of the city.
  • The industrial city still focused on the city center, which contained both the central business district, defined by large office buildings,
    and substantial numbers of factory and warehouse structures. Both trolleys and railroad systems converged on the center
    of the city, which boasted the premier entertainment and shopping establishments.
    The working class lived in crowded districts close to the city center, near their place of employment.
